Chapter One · Foundations

What Actually Is Muscle?

Before you can build something, you need to understand what it is. Most people go to the gym for years without ever knowing what muscle actually is at a biological level. That's like trying to fix a machine without knowing how it works. Let's fix that.

Your body has three types of muscle tissue: cardiac muscle (the heart — involuntary, never tires), smooth muscle (organs and blood vessels — also involuntary), and skeletal muscle — the kind you train. When people say "building muscle," they mean skeletal muscle exclusively.

The Structure of Skeletal Muscle

Think of muscle like a cable made of smaller cables, all bundled together. The largest unit is the whole muscle itself — say, your bicep. Inside that muscle are bundles called fascicles. Inside each fascicle are individual muscle fibres (these are actual cells — very long, cylindrical ones). Inside each fibre are myofibrils. And inside myofibrils are the smallest functional units: sarcomeres.

Muscle Architecture — From Large to Small
01
Whole Muscle (e.g. Bicep Brachii)
The organ you see. Made of thousands of muscle fibres bundled together. Surrounded by connective tissue called the epimysium.
02
Fascicle
A bundle of 10–100 muscle fibres grouped together. Surrounded by perimysium. Like a small sub-cable inside the main cable.
03
Muscle Fibre (Cell)
A single, giant, multi-nucleated cell. Can be several centimetres long. This is the unit that actually contracts. Surrounded by endomysium.
04
Myofibril
Thread-like structures inside the fibre. Each fibre contains thousands. They are the contractile machinery — the actual engine.
05
Sarcomere
The smallest unit of contraction. Made of two proteins — actin (thin filaments) and myosin (thick filaments). When these two slide past each other, contraction happens.

The Sliding Filament Theory — How Muscle Contracts

This is the foundational mechanism of all muscular movement, and it's elegant in its simplicity. When your brain sends a signal to contract a muscle, here is exactly what happens at the molecular level:

The myosin heads (which look like tiny golf clubs) reach out and attach to the actin filaments in what are called cross-bridges. They then pivot — like oars rowing — pulling the actin filaments toward the centre of the sarcomere. This shortens the sarcomere. Thousands of sarcomeres shortening simultaneously shortens the whole muscle. That's a contraction. That's a bicep curl. That's every movement your body makes.

The Energy Currency — ATP

Every single cross-bridge cycle requires energy in the form of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). ATP is the universal energy currency of cells. When myosin grabs actin and pulls, it splits one ATP molecule to get the energy to do so. This is why exercise depletes energy — you are literally burning through ATP at a rapid rate.

Your body has three systems to regenerate ATP: the phosphocreatine system (lasts ~10 seconds, used in explosive lifts), glycolysis (lasts 30 seconds to 2 minutes, used in most weight training sets), and oxidative phosphorylation (aerobic, used in long cardio). Understanding this explains why rest between sets matters — you're waiting for ATP to be replenished.

Chapter Two · Foundations

How Muscle Actually Grows

Muscle growth is called hypertrophy. The word comes from Greek: hyper (excess) + trophe (nourishment). It specifically means an increase in the size of existing muscle cells — not the creation of new ones. This distinction matters enormously.

Hypertrophy vs. Hyperplasia
Hypertrophy = existing muscle cells getting bigger (this is what weight training causes in humans). Hyperplasia = new muscle cells being created (this happens in some animals but is not meaningfully demonstrated in humans from normal training). When you "build muscle," you are enlarging the fibres you already have — not growing new ones.

So what makes a muscle fibre get bigger? The answer is a process called muscle protein synthesis (MPS). Here is the complete, accurate version of that process:

The Complete Loop of Muscle Growth
1
Mechanical Tension & Damage
You lift a weight. The muscle fibres experience mechanical stress. Some of the contractile proteins (actin and myosin) are disrupted or damaged at the microscopic level. This is normal and necessary.
2
Inflammatory Response
The damage triggers an immune response. Satellite cells (muscle stem cells that sit on the outside of fibres) are activated. This is why muscles feel tender or sore 24–48 hours later — DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness).
3
Satellite Cell Activation
Satellite cells fuse with existing muscle fibres and donate their nuclei. More nuclei means the fibre can produce more protein. This is how fibres expand their capacity for growth.
4
mTOR Signalling
The mammalian Target of Rapamycin (mTOR) is a key signalling protein activated by exercise and protein intake. It tells the cell's ribosomes to ramp up protein production. This is the molecular "ON switch" for muscle growth.
5
Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS)
Ribosomes inside the muscle cell read genetic instructions and assemble amino acids (from food) into new contractile proteins — primarily actin and myosin. These are added to existing myofibrils, making them thicker.
6
Net Positive Protein Balance
If MPS exceeds MPB (Muscle Protein Breakdown), the fibre gets bigger. This requires sufficient protein from diet AND adequate rest time. Growth happens at rest, not in the gym.

"You do not grow muscle in the gym. You damage muscle in the gym. You grow muscle during sleep and rest — when your body has the time and raw materials to rebuild."

Fundamental principle of exercise physiology

What DOMS Really Is

Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness — the soreness you feel 24–72 hours after a hard session — is not lactic acid (a persistent myth). It is primarily caused by microscopic tears in the muscle fibres and connective tissue, along with the subsequent inflammatory response. It peaks at 24–48 hours because inflammation takes time to develop. DOMS being present does not mean you had a good workout. DOMS being absent does not mean you had a bad one. It is simply a side effect of novel or intense training, which diminishes as your body adapts.

Not all muscle fibres are identical. They exist on a spectrum based on their speed of contraction, fatigue resistance, and how much energy they use. Understanding this explains why different rep ranges and training styles produce different results.

The practical takeaway: To maximally stimulate all fibre types, you need a mix of rep ranges in your training. Training determines how close you get to it.

Chapter Four · Training Science

The Three Stimuli for Growth

Exercise science has identified three primary mechanisms by which resistance training stimulates muscle growth. They are not equally important — but they all contribute, and a well-designed program addresses all three.

1. Mechanical Tension

This is the dominant driver of hypertrophy and the most scientifically supported mechanism. When a muscle contracts against a load, the force generated creates mechanical tension across the muscle fibre. This tension is detected by proteins called mechanosensors embedded in the cell membrane, which then trigger the mTOR signalling cascade described earlier.

The key insight here is that tension must be applied across the full range of motion, and it must be sufficient (i.e., the weight must be challenging). A light weight lifted for 100 reps generates less effective tension than a moderate weight taken to near-failure for 12 reps, because the force output and fibre recruitment are much lower.

→
Stretched tension is especially powerful. Research (Pedrosa et al., 2022) shows that muscles loaded at their stretched position — the bottom of a squat, the bottom of a fly, the stretched position of a Romanian deadlift — produce significantly greater hypertrophy. This is why full range of motion is not just about safety; it's about maximal growth stimulus.

2. Metabolic Stress

When you perform higher rep sets (12–20 reps) with shorter rest periods, you accumulate metabolic by-products in the muscle: lactate, hydrogen ions, and inorganic phosphate. This creates the "pump" (called transient hypertrophy — a temporary swelling of the muscle with blood and fluid) and the burning sensation you feel.

Metabolic stress triggers the release of anabolic hormones locally, causes cell swelling (which signals the cell to grow in order to prevent future swelling), and recruits additional motor units. Whether it causes growth independently or purely as a result of the tension it creates is still debated — but practically, it contributes. Higher-rep work with shorter rest targets this mechanism.

3. Muscle Damage

When you train in an unusual way, train a muscle it isn't used to, or emphasise the eccentric phase (the lowering/lengthening phase of a movement), you create more microscopic damage than normal. This triggers the satellite cell response described in Chapter 2, and the repair process results in a slightly larger fibre.

However — and this is important — muscle damage is not necessary for growth and should not be maximised. Excessive damage delays recovery and reduces the frequency with which you can train. Experienced lifters progressively reduce muscle damage as they adapt, which is actually a good thing — it means they recover faster.

"Soreness does not equal growth. A well-adapted lifter barely gets sore and grows continuously. A beginner is always sore and grows rapidly because everything is new. Soreness is a novelty signal, not a success signal."

Exercise Physiology Principle
Chapter Five · Training Science

Reps, Sets, Volume & Intensity

These are the variables you control every time you step into the gym. Understanding what each one means scientifically — not just as gym jargon — is what separates people who train intelligently from people who just go through the motions.

Definitions

Repetition (Rep)
One complete cycle of an exercise — concentric (muscle shortens, e.g. lifting the bar) + eccentric (muscle lengthens, e.g. lowering the bar). One full up-and-down of a curl = one rep.
Set
A group of consecutive repetitions performed without stopping. "3 sets of 10" means you do 10 reps, rest, do 10 reps, rest, do 10 reps.
Volume
Total work done for a muscle. Calculated as Sets × Reps × Weight. Example: 3 sets × 10 reps × 60kg = 1800kg of volume. Volume is the most important training variable for hypertrophy.
Intensity
In proper scientific terminology, intensity means the percentage of your 1 Rep Max (1RM) you are lifting. Not how hard it "feels." Lifting 80kg on an exercise where your max is 100kg = 80% intensity.
RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)
A scale from 1–10 describing how close to failure a set is. RPE 10 = you couldn't do another rep. RPE 8 = you had 2 reps left in the tank. Most working sets should be RPE 7–9. RPE 6 or below = not challenging enough to drive growth.

The Rep Range Spectrum & What Research Says

For years, gym culture insisted that 8–12 reps was the "hypertrophy range" and lower reps were for strength, higher reps for endurance. The science has evolved. Research by Schoenfeld et al. (2017) and others has demonstrated that:

Muscle can grow across a wide rep range — as low as 5 and as high as 30+ — provided the sets are taken close to muscular failure. The key variable is proximity to failure, not the specific rep number. A set of 5 and a set of 20 can produce equivalent hypertrophy if both are taken to ~2 reps from failure.

However, lower reps (3–6) produce greater absolute strength gains because they develop the nervous system's ability to recruit motor units maximally. Higher reps (15–30) produce more metabolic stress and are better for some isolation exercises where heavy loads risk joint injury. The ideal approach uses a combination — which is exactly what your PPL plan does.

Rep Range Primary Adaptation Best Use Note
1–5 reps Neural strength, max power Powerlifts (squat, deadlift, bench) High CNS demand. Not ideal for beginners.
6–12 reps Hypertrophy + strength Most compound exercises Best balance for muscle building.
12–20 reps Hypertrophy + endurance Isolation exercises, joints High metabolic stress. Lower injury risk.
20–30+ reps Endurance, metabolic Calves, forearms, rehab Still builds muscle if taken near failure.

How Much Volume Per Week?

Research on weekly volume per muscle group (Krieger, Schoenfeld) suggests a dose-response relationship: more volume generally produces more growth, up to a recoverable maximum. For a beginner, 10–15 working sets per muscle per week is a good starting point. For intermediate, 15–20. Going beyond 20 sets requires strong recovery (sleep, nutrition) to avoid overtraining.

Chapter Six · Training Science

Progressive Overload — The Master Principle

If there is one principle above all others that determines whether you grow or stagnate, it is progressive overload. Every other variable — rep range, exercise selection, rest periods — is secondary to this.

Progressive Overload
Progressively increasing the demand placed on the musculoskeletal system over time, so the body is continually forced to adapt. Without progression, the body has no reason to change. It has already adapted to the current stimulus.

This principle was formalised by Dr. Thomas DeLorme in the 1940s after working with injured soldiers during World War II. He found that progressively increasing the load was the most effective way to rebuild muscle strength. The science behind it is the stress-recovery-adaptation cycle.

The Stress–Recovery–Adaptation Cycle (Supercompensation)
S
Stress (Training)
You apply a training stimulus. Performance temporarily drops. The body is fatigued. Muscle proteins are disrupted. This is necessary disruption.
R
Recovery (Rest & Nutrition)
With adequate rest and protein, the body repairs the damage. It restores baseline function. This takes 48–72 hours for most muscle groups.
A
Adaptation (Supercompensation)
The body overshoots the repair — it doesn't just restore baseline, it builds slightly above it. This is the window where you are stronger than before. If you train again in this window, you progress.
!
If you miss the window
If you wait too long, the adaptation disappears and you return to baseline. If you train too soon, you compound fatigue without growth. Frequency matters.

Ways to Apply Progressive Overload

Most people think progressive overload only means adding weight. That is one way. There are several valid methods, and you should cycle through them:

Method Example When to Use
Add Weight 60kg → 62.5kg on bench Most common. Best for compound lifts.
Add Reps 3×8 → 3×10 same weight When you've mastered current weight.
Add Sets 3 sets → 4 sets When you can recover more volume.
Reduce Rest 3 min rest → 2 min rest For metabolic stress, conditioning.
Improve Tempo Slower eccentric (2s → 4s) Increases time under tension without more weight.
Improve Range of Motion Deeper squat Often more important than adding weight.
Chapter Seven · Training Science

Compound vs. Isolation Exercises

This is one of the most debated topics in gym culture. Understanding the science makes the answer straightforward: you need both, but for different reasons — and their priority order matters.

Compound Exercise
A multi-joint movement that recruits multiple muscle groups simultaneously. Examples: squat (quads, glutes, hamstrings, lower back, core), bench press (pec, anterior delt, tricep), deadlift (entire posterior chain), row (lats, rhomboids, biceps, rear delts).
Isolation Exercise
A single-joint movement targeting one primary muscle. Examples: bicep curl (bicep only), lateral raise (medial delt only), leg extension (quad only), calf raise (gastrocnemius only).
Factor Compound Isolation
Muscles targeted Multiple simultaneously One primarily
Load potential Very high (heavy weights) Moderate
Hormonal response Higher (more muscle mass involved) Lower
Caloric burn High Low
Skill requirement High (form is critical) Low to moderate
Best for Foundation of program, strength base Finishing, targeting weak points
→
The rule: Compound exercises are done first in every session (fresh, maximum energy, maximum load). Isolation work is done at the end to finish off the target muscle. A program built only on isolation exercises will produce inferior results to one built on compounds first.
Chapter Eight · Training Science

Rest, Recovery & Sleep

This is the chapter most people ignore, and it is the reason most people do not grow despite training consistently. Recovery is not passive. It is an active biological process that requires time and resources — and you cannot shortcut it.

Muscle Protein Synthesis Timeline

After a training session, muscle protein synthesis (MPS) elevates above baseline for approximately 24–48 hours in trained individuals (up to 72 hours in beginners). This is the window during which growth actually occurs. If you train the same muscle again before this window closes, you disrupt the repair process. If you wait too long after it closes, you miss the supercompensation peak.

This is why the 48–72 hour gap between training the same muscle group is standard. Your PPL plan does exactly this — Push on Monday means chest/shoulders get their 72+ hours before Push again on Saturday.

Sleep — The Non-Negotiable

During sleep, particularly during slow-wave (deep) sleep, the pituitary gland releases the majority of the day's growth hormone (GH). Growth hormone is a primary anabolic signal — it stimulates muscle protein synthesis, promotes fat oxidation, and regulates muscle repair. Without adequate deep sleep, this GH pulse is diminished.

Research by Dattilo et al. (2011) demonstrated that sleep deprivation increases cortisol (a catabolic hormone that breaks muscle down) and decreases testosterone and IGF-1 (anabolic hormones). In practical terms: sleeping 5–6 hours when you need 7–8 puts you in a state where your hormonal environment actively works against muscle growth. Training hard on chronic poor sleep is almost pointless.

Active Recovery

Rest days do not mean complete inactivity. Light movement (walking, stretching, mobility work) on rest days improves blood flow to recovering muscles, which accelerates nutrient delivery and waste product removal. This is called active recovery and reduces next-day soreness without adding training stress.

Chapter Nine · Nutrition Science

Protein & Amino Acids

Protein is the structural raw material of muscle. Without it, MPS cannot occur regardless of how hard you train. Understanding protein at a deeper level than "eat more chicken" will help you make better decisions.

What Protein Actually Is

Protein is a macromolecule made of chains of amino acids. There are 20 amino acids total. Nine of them are essential amino acids (EAAs) — your body cannot synthesise them, so they must come from food. Of those nine, three are especially critical for muscle protein synthesis: leucine, isoleucine, and valine — collectively called Branched Chain Amino Acids (BCAAs), with leucine being the primary trigger for mTOR activation.

The Leucine Threshold

Research has identified a "leucine threshold" — approximately 2–3 grams of leucine per meal is required to maximally stimulate MPS. Below this threshold, MPS is suboptimally activated even if total protein is adequate. This is why protein quality (i.e., leucine content) matters, not just quantity.

Animal proteins (eggs, chicken, fish, beef, dairy/paneer, whey) have high leucine content and are "complete proteins" — they contain all 9 EAAs. Plant proteins (lentils, rice, beans) often lack one or more EAAs and have lower leucine. This doesn't mean plant proteins are useless — but vegetarians/vegans need to eat larger quantities and combine sources.

How Much Protein?

The current scientific consensus (based on meta-analyses by Morton et al., 2018) is that muscle protein synthesis is maximally stimulated at approximately 1.6g of protein per kg of bodyweight per day, with some benefit up to 2.2g/kg. At 82kg, that is 131–180g of protein daily. Going higher has no proven additional benefit for muscle growth (the excess is simply oxidised for energy).

Protein Distribution — Timing Across Meals

Research (Areta et al., 2013) shows that distributing protein across 3–5 meals produces greater MPS than eating the same total protein in one or two meals. This is because each meal needs to hit the leucine threshold, and the MPS response is transient — it peaks and returns to baseline within 3–4 hours even if amino acids are still elevated. Practically: aim for 30–45g of quality protein per meal, 3–4 times a day.

Chapter Ten · Nutrition Science

Calories, Energy Balance & Body Composition

The question of whether to eat more or less is determined by what you want — and understanding energy balance at a physiological level will tell you exactly why.

TDEE — Total Daily Energy Expenditure
The total calories your body burns in a day across all activities. It is the sum of: Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR — calories burned at rest), Thermic Effect of Food (TEF — energy cost of digesting food, ~10% of intake), Exercise Activity (gym + cardio), and NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — walking, fidgeting, all other movement).
Goal Calorie Target What Happens Risk
Bulk (muscle gain) TDEE + 200–400 kcal Muscle grows fast, some fat gained Excess fat gain if surplus too large
Cut (fat loss) TDEE − 300–500 kcal Fat lost, muscle partially preserved Muscle loss if deficit too large or protein low
Recomposition TDEE − 100–200 kcal Slow fat loss + slow muscle gain Slow progress. Requires patience.
Maintenance = TDEE Weight stays stable Limited muscle growth without surplus

For you at 82kg as a beginner: recomposition is achievable and is the right choice. Beginners have a unique advantage — they can gain muscle and lose fat simultaneously because their muscles respond strongly to the novel stimulus of training even in a slight deficit, and their fat stores provide energy to fuel the process. This window lasts approximately 6–12 months before you need to choose one goal explicitly.

Chapter Eleven · Nutrition Science

Carbohydrates & Fats

After protein, the most important dietary question is how to distribute your remaining calories between carbohydrates and fats. Both have specific physiological roles, and both are necessary.

Carbohydrates — The Primary Fuel

Carbohydrates are broken down into glucose, which is stored in muscle and liver as glycogen. Glycogen is the primary fuel source during high-intensity exercise (weight training is high-intensity anaerobic exercise). When glycogen is depleted, performance drops significantly — you fatigue faster, you can't maintain intensity, and MPS is impaired.

This is why very low-carb (ketogenic) diets produce poor strength and hypertrophy outcomes for most people — the fuel required for the exercise simply isn't available. You do not need to eat high carb, but eating adequate carbs (especially around training) is important for performance and growth.

Fats — Hormonal Foundation

Dietary fat is the precursor to steroid hormones, including testosterone. Chronically very low fat intake (below ~20% of total calories) suppresses testosterone production. Since testosterone is one of the primary anabolic hormones driving muscle growth, this is a significant problem. Fats also provide fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) essential for overall health and hormonal function.

A practical split for your goals: after hitting your protein target (let's say 140g = 560 kcal), split the remaining calories roughly 50–55% carbohydrates and 25–30% fats. Don't obsess over this — hitting protein is the priority. Carbs and fats are secondary.

Chapter Twelve · Advanced

Hormones — The Internal Environment

Your hormonal environment is the biological context in which all muscle growth either occurs or fails to occur. You cannot directly control your hormones the way you control a dumbbell — but you can create conditions that optimise or destroy them through lifestyle choices.

Testosterone

The primary male anabolic hormone. Binds to androgen receptors in muscle cells and directly upregulates muscle protein synthesis and satellite cell activation. Higher testosterone = more responsive muscles. Testosterone is naturally elevated by: heavy compound training (especially squats and deadlifts, which involve large muscle mass), adequate sleep, dietary fat, and maintaining healthy body fat levels. It is suppressed by: chronic sleep deprivation, chronic caloric deficit, chronic stress, excessive alcohol, and very high body fat.

Growth Hormone (GH) & IGF-1

GH is released primarily during deep sleep. It stimulates the liver to produce IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1), which then directly stimulates muscle cell proliferation and MPS. High-volume training and deep sleep maximise GH release. GH also promotes fat oxidation — it preferentially mobilises fat for energy, which is why sleep is important not just for muscle but for fat loss too.

Cortisol — The Catabolic Hormone

Cortisol is the primary stress hormone. It is catabolic — meaning it breaks things down, including muscle protein, to provide the body with glucose in a stress situation. Cortisol spikes naturally during training (it's part of the acute response) and comes down during recovery. Chronically elevated cortisol — from sleep deprivation, psychological stress, over-training, or severe caloric restriction — actively opposes muscle growth and promotes fat storage (particularly visceral abdominal fat).

→
The practical summary of hormones: Sleep 7–8 hours (GH + testosterone), manage stress (cortisol), eat enough fat (testosterone precursors), train with heavy compound movements (testosterone spike), don't over-train (cortisol management). These lifestyle factors are your hormonal levers. Supplements pale in comparison to getting these right.

Insulin — The Nutrient Distributor

Insulin is released when blood glucose rises (after carbohydrate intake). Its primary role is to drive glucose into cells. Crucially for muscle building, insulin also inhibits muscle protein breakdown (MPB) — it is anti-catabolic. This is why eating carbohydrates around training (pre and post workout) is useful — it keeps insulin elevated during the recovery window, reducing the catabolic environment and supporting net protein balance.

Chapter Thirteen · Advanced

The Nervous System & Strength

Strength is not the same thing as muscle size. Understanding the distinction explains why powerlifters can be phenomenally strong without looking massively muscular, and why bodybuilders can look huge but underperform in maximal strength tests.

Motor Units & Recruitment

A motor unit is a motor neuron plus all the muscle fibres it controls. When the brain sends a signal to produce force, it recruits motor units according to the Size Principle (Henneman, 1965): smaller, lower-threshold motor units are recruited first. As required force increases, larger, higher-threshold motor units (controlling fast-twitch fibres) are progressively recruited. Maximum force requires maximum motor unit recruitment.

Early strength gains in beginners (weeks 1–8) are almost entirely neural adaptations — the nervous system learns to recruit more motor units simultaneously, to synchronise them, and to reduce inhibitory signals. Very little muscle is actually built in the first 4–6 weeks. This is why beginners get significantly stronger without looking noticeably bigger. It's neurological learning.

The Mind-Muscle Connection

Research (Calatayud et al., 2016; Schoenfeld & Contreras, 2016) has demonstrated that actively focusing on contracting the target muscle during an exercise — the so-called mind-muscle connection — increases activation of that muscle during the movement. For isolation exercises like bicep curls and lateral raises, this focus can increase muscle activation by 10–20% compared to simply moving the weight. For compound lifts (squat, deadlift), external focus (focus on the movement or the bar) tends to produce better overall performance. Practically: think about the muscle you're training during isolation exercises. Don't just push the weight — squeeze the muscle.

Chapter Fourteen · Advanced

Adaptation, Plateaus & Periodisation

Your body is a survival machine. Its entire purpose is to adapt to whatever stress you impose on it and become efficient enough that the same stress no longer challenges it. This is the engine of progress — and also the source of every plateau.

The Law of Accommodation

Also called the Repeated Bout Effect: the more familiar your body is with a given stimulus, the less it responds to it. The first time you squat heavy, MPS spikes significantly. The hundredth time you squat the same weight, MPS barely moves above baseline. Your body has already built the machinery to handle that load efficiently. This is why progressive overload is not optional — it is the mechanism that prevents accommodation.

Periodisation — Structured Variation

Periodisation is the intentional planning of training variation over time to maximise long-term adaptation and prevent accommodation. It was developed by Soviet sports scientists in the 1950s–70s. There are several models:

Linear periodisation means gradually increasing load over weeks while decreasing reps (e.g., week 1: 3×12, week 4: 3×8, week 8: 3×5). Good for beginners.

Undulating periodisation varies volume and intensity within a week or even within a single session. More advanced — it prevents accommodation faster by constantly changing the stimulus.

Deloading is a planned reduction in volume/intensity (typically week 7–8 of a training block) to allow full systemic recovery and allow supercompensation to peak. A deload week at 50–60% intensity is not laziness — it is strategy. Many people avoid deloads and then wonder why their progress stalls after 8 weeks.

"The body adapts to stress, not to training. Give it the same stress indefinitely, and adaptation stops. Give it no stress, and there is nothing to adapt to. The art is in the calibration."

Principle of Sport Science

Glossary of Key Terms

Every term you will encounter in gym literature, research, or conversation — defined plainly and accurately.

1RM (One Rep Max)
The maximum weight you can lift for exactly one complete repetition. Used to calculate training percentages.
ATP
Adenosine Triphosphate. The universal energy currency of cells. Every muscular contraction costs ATP.
BCAAs
Branched Chain Amino Acids (leucine, isoleucine, valine). The most anabolic amino acids, particularly leucine.
BMR
Basal Metabolic Rate. Calories burned at complete rest just to sustain life (breathing, organ function, etc.).
Catabolic
Breakdown state. Breaking complex molecules into simpler ones. Opposite of anabolic. Cortisol is catabolic.
Compound Lift
Multi-joint exercise recruiting multiple muscle groups. Squat, deadlift, bench press, row, overhead press.
Concentric
The shortening phase of a muscle contraction. Lifting the bar up in a curl = concentric.
Cortisol
Primary stress hormone. Catabolic in excess. Breaks down muscle protein to provide glucose in stressful situations.
Deload
A planned training week at reduced volume and intensity (~50–60%) to allow full recovery and supercompensation.
DOMS
Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness. Muscle tenderness 24–72h post-training caused by microscopic fibre damage and inflammation.
Eccentric
The lengthening/lowering phase of a contraction. Lowering the bar in a curl = eccentric. Most muscle damage occurs here.
Fascicle
A bundle of muscle fibres grouped together within a muscle. Like a sub-cable inside the main muscle cable.
Glycogen
The stored form of glucose in muscles and liver. Primary fuel for high-intensity exercise including weight training.
Hypertrophy
Increase in muscle cell size. What "building muscle" means at the cellular level. Not the creation of new cells.
IGF-1
Insulin-like Growth Factor 1. Produced by the liver in response to growth hormone. Directly stimulates MPS.
Isometric
Muscle contraction with no change in length. Holding a plank or the midpoint of a curl. No joint movement.
MPS
Muscle Protein Synthesis. The process of building new muscle proteins using amino acids. The mechanism of growth.
Motor Unit
A motor neuron and all the muscle fibres it innervates. The functional unit of muscle recruitment.
mTOR
Mammalian Target of Rapamycin. The primary molecular signalling complex that triggers MPS. Activated by exercise and leucine.
NEAT
Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis. Calories burned through all movement that isn't formal exercise. Walking, fidgeting, etc.
Progressive Overload
Progressively increasing training demand over time to continuously force adaptation. The master principle of all training.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion. Scale of 1–10 measuring how close to failure a set is. RPE 10 = absolute failure.
Sarcomere
The smallest contractile unit of muscle. Made of actin and myosin filaments that slide past each other to generate force.
Satellite Cells
Muscle stem cells that activate after training damage. They fuse with fibres and donate nuclei, enabling greater growth.
TDEE
Total Daily Energy Expenditure. Total calories your body burns per day across all activity and biological function.
Tempo
The speed of each phase of a rep. Written as eccentric-pause-concentric (e.g. 3-0-1 = 3s down, no pause, 1s up).
Time Under Tension (TUT)
Total duration a muscle is loaded during a set. Slower tempos increase TUT, increasing mechanical tension stimulus.
Training Volume
Total work done = Sets × Reps × Weight. The most important training variable for hypertrophy according to current research.
