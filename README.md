# The Science of Muscle — A Complete Guide

> A scientifically grounded, beginner-to-advanced reference on how muscle works, how it grows, and what you must do to make it grow. No gym-bro myth. No fluff. Just the biology.

---

## What This Is

This is a self-contained, interactive HTML guide covering the complete science of skeletal muscle — from molecular biology to training programming to nutrition. It was built for someone who wants real answers backed by exercise physiology research, not recycled fitness influencer advice.

The guide is a single `.html` file. Open it in any browser. No dependencies, no installation, no internet required.

---

## Contents

### Foundations
- **Chapter 1 — What Actually Is Muscle?**
  Skeletal muscle structure, the sliding filament theory, sarcomeres, actin and myosin, and how ATP powers every contraction.

- **Chapter 2 — How Muscle Actually Grows**
  Hypertrophy vs hyperplasia, muscle protein synthesis (MPS), the mTOR signalling pathway, satellite cells, and the complete growth loop.

- **Chapter 3 — Muscle Fibre Types**
  Type I, Type IIa, Type IIx — their properties, size potential, best rep ranges, and what genetics does and doesn't determine.

### Training Science
- **Chapter 4 — The Three Stimuli for Growth**
  Mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and muscle damage — how each works and which matters most.

- **Chapter 5 — Reps, Sets, Volume & Intensity**
  What these terms actually mean scientifically (not gym-bro definitions), the rep range spectrum, RPE, and weekly volume targets.

- **Chapter 6 — Progressive Overload**
  The master principle. The stress-recovery-adaptation cycle, supercompensation, and six distinct ways to apply overload beyond just adding weight.

- **Chapter 7 — Compound vs. Isolation Exercises**
  The mechanical and hormonal differences, when to use each, and why exercise order matters.

- **Chapter 8 — Rest, Recovery & Sleep**
  The MPS timeline, why growth hormone peaks during deep sleep, cortisol management, and active recovery.

### Nutrition Science
- **Chapter 9 — Protein & Amino Acids**
  Essential amino acids, leucine threshold, complete vs incomplete proteins, how much protein you actually need, and meal distribution.

- **Chapter 10 — Calories, Energy Balance & Body Composition**
  TDEE, BMR, NEAT, bulking vs cutting vs recomposition — and why beginners can do both simultaneously.

- **Chapter 11 — Carbohydrates & Fats**
  Glycogen as training fuel, why low-carb hurts performance, and why dietary fat is critical for testosterone production.

### Advanced
- **Chapter 12 — Hormones**
  Testosterone, growth hormone, IGF-1, cortisol, and insulin — what each does and how lifestyle controls them.

- **Chapter 13 — The Nervous System & Strength**
  Motor units, the Size Principle, why early strength gains are neural not muscular, and the science of the mind-muscle connection.

- **Chapter 14 — Adaptation, Plateaus & Periodisation**
  The Law of Accommodation, linear vs undulating periodisation, and why deloading is strategy not laziness.

### Reference
- **Glossary** — 28 key terms defined accurately: 1RM, ATP, BCAAs, DOMS, mTOR, RPE, sarcomere, TDEE, and more.

## Key Research Referenced

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Schoenfeld et al. (2017) | Muscle grows across a wide rep range (5–30+) if taken near failure |
| Morton et al. (2018) | MPS maximised at ~1.6g protein/kg/day, benefit up to 2.2g/kg |
| Pedrosa et al. (2022) | Stretched-position loading produces significantly greater hypertrophy |
| Areta et al. (2013) | Distributing protein across 3–5 meals produces greater MPS than 1–2 meals |
| Dattilo et al. (2011) | Sleep deprivation raises cortisol and lowers testosterone and IGF-1 |
| Calatayud et al. (2016) | Mind-muscle focus increases isolation exercise activation by 10–20% |
| Henneman (1965) | Size Principle of motor unit recruitment |
